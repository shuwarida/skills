Professional design starts not with "is it beautiful?" but with "what will the viewer actually understand?". Aesthetics are subjective — confusion is objective. If a design has to be explained, it has failed. And users aren't making logical decisions: defaults read as recommendations, the first number sets the ruler, a gift creates a debt, building creates ownership, progress creates momentum.

## How to run: grill, don't lecture

This skill is an interview, not a checklist to recite. Grill the user relentlessly through the principles below until you reach a shared understanding of the design:

- Walk the tree in order — Part I before any pixel exists, Part II for each screen or flow that asks the user to decide, act, or sign up. Skip principles that genuinely don't apply (no pricing → no contrast question).
- Ask one question at a time and wait for the answer. Multiple questions at once are bewildering.
- For every question, give your recommended answer, grounded in the principle you're applying.
- A *fact* you can find yourself (existing screens, copy, analytics, the codebase) — look it up rather than asking. The *decisions* are the user's — put each one to them and wait.
- Do not start designing until the user confirms shared understanding.

# Part I — Pre-brief: think before you design

## 1. Perception gap

The gap is the distance between what the design is supposed to communicate and what the viewer actually took away. Before evaluating any layout, ask three questions:

- Where does the eye land first?
- What gets noticed second?
- What gets missed entirely?

**Test:** show the layout for a few seconds and ask the person to describe from memory the *sequence* of what they saw, not the details. If the first thing named is wrong — the hierarchy failed. If the call to action was missed — the design didn't guide the viewer.

## 2. The viewer's starting emotional state

Before you begin, answer: what psychological state is the person in when they arrive at the design? A charity website visitor is cautious and looking for trust; someone flipping through a fashion magazine is relaxed and not thinking hard. The design must meet the person where they already are emotionally — otherwise something immediately feels "off".

Account for the physical context too: the real viewer is a tired person on a phone with the brightness turned down, not a designer inspecting kerning. Design for people with no design background.

## 3. Predictive empathy

Design not for what the viewer feels now, but for what they should feel a few seconds later and carry away with them. Techniques:

- Soften the headline's tone while keeping its strength — don't "grab them by the arm".
- Add breathing room to the composition.
- Insert one emotional signal: a line of reassurance or a trust symbol.
- Steer the palette toward the target emotional state.

Every decision must have a reason, and one of those reasons is the emotion the viewer leaves with.

## 4. Memory encoding

A design is remembered when the brain is given something to *resolve*: a conceptual twist, a slight imbalance, a question instead of an answer (vinyl records arranged into a heart; empty clothes on a chair as a figure without a person). The pause for making sense of it is memory formation itself. Anything too safe, clean, and unoriginal evaporates from the mind the moment the scroll continues.

## 5. Brand voice before visuals (for identity and logos)

A brand lives in captions, landing pages, push notifications, and tweets — don't start with geometry. First, in writing:

1. Five words the brand would *never* say — these are the boundaries.
2. Five words the brand *overuses* online — this is the tone (sharp? playful? technical?).
3. One sentence the brand would use to introduce itself to the world in 140 characters.

The voice sets the emotional temperature, and the logo must match it: a bold brand doesn't get a timid mark, a calm one doesn't get aggressive angles.

## 6. The experience around the work

Perception creates value before the design itself: how the file opens, how the layers are organized and named, how the presentation is structured. The same project sent as a "bare PDF" versus walked through with a clear structure is two different values for the same work. The client pays for confidence, clarity, and the ease of the whole experience, not just the picture.

# Part II — Behavioral principles for screens and flows

## 7. Smart defaults

Pre-select the most common choice for every field. Defaults read as recommendations — "this is what most people pick" — and 70–90% of users never change them. The user's job shifts from "fill this out from scratch" to "scan and adjust what doesn't fit", a fundamentally easier task. Fewer open decisions means more completions (jam study: 24 flavors → 3% bought; 6 flavors → 30%). Let the button carry the outcome too: "Show 12 results", not "Search".

## 8. Goal gradient

Never start a user at zero. Count something they've already done as step one — account creation, the first pre-checked item — so progress opens at 20%, not 0%. The closer people feel to the finish, the faster they move toward it (loyalty card with 2 of 10 stamps pre-filled: nearly double the completion of an empty 8-stamp card). Zero feels like standing still; a head start feels like momentum, and that feeling is what separates finishers from second-screen drop-offs.

## 9. Reciprocity

Give value before asking for anything. Deliver a genuinely useful partial result first — their score, top issues, what passed — then ask: "Want the complete breakdown? Save your report." Blurring results behind "create an account to see" is holding them hostage: a restaurant demanding your card before showing the menu. A received gift creates an unconscious debt (the strongest driver of persuasion per Cialdini; free samples lift purchases up to 2,000%), so the sign-up never feels like a wall — the user already got something worth coming back for.

## 10. Endowment (IKEA effect)

Let users build before they sign up. Choosing a name, a palette, a card style — each choice makes the thing *theirs*, and people value what they built far above an identical thing made by someone else; even just feeling ownership is enough. By the time the sign-up screen appears, leaving no longer means skipping a form — it means abandoning something they made (Duolingo: language picked, goal set, first lesson done before any account). The button says "Continue", not "Sign up".

## 11. Loss aversion

When a screen asks users to act, flip the framing: don't sell what they'll gain, show what they'll lose if they don't. Losing weighs roughly twice as much as gaining the same thing (Kahneman), and status quo bias makes people protect what they already have. Show the concrete stakes by name — *their* files, with a countdown — and make the dismiss option own the risk ("I'll risk it"), never a free escape hatch ("Maybe later"). A pitch has zero psychological weight; the felt cost of inaction wins.

## 12. Contrast effect

Never show a cost in isolation. The brain evaluates every number relative to the one it just saw, so control what the user sees first — that first number becomes the ruler. A $50/mo plan on its own page reads as "$600 a year"; the same $50 under a $1,900 laptop with a "just 2.6%" label reads as a rounding error. Same offer, different anchor (the $90 steak exists to make the $40 salmon reasonable).
